01Introduction to Art

What is Art & Its Significance

Art has no simple definition. At its core, art represents human expression made visible, audible, or tangible through creative processes. Philosopher Arthur Danto argued that art involves both an object and the theoretical framework that gives it meaning — art exists within interpretive contexts, not as isolated objects.

General Classification of the Arts

🖼️

Visual Arts

Painting, sculpture, photography, digital art — experienced primarily through sight.

📖

Literary Arts

Poetry, fiction, drama — engage the imagination through written or spoken language and storytelling.

🎭

Performing Arts

Music, dance, theater — meaning created through live or recorded performance over time.

Significance of Art

To Individuals

Self-expression — articulates thoughts and feelings that might otherwise remain unexpressed. A person struggling with grief may find solace in music; joy might be expressed through dance or visual creation.

To Society

Cultural Identity — indigenous art, traditional music, regional architecture all contribute to distinctive cultural identities across generations.

Social Connection

Concerts, museums, theater, and online communities demonstrate how shared artistic experiences bring people together, building unity.

Historical Record

Art preserves information about past societies. Cave paintings reveal prehistoric life; medieval manuscripts show social hierarchies; street art documents current movements.

Art in Everyday Life

  • Music enhances daily activities like commuting, working, and relaxing
  • Fashion reflects creativity, culture, and personal identity
  • Social media features visual arts through photos, videos, and design
  • Films and advertisements combine arts to entertain and influence society
02Module 2, Lesson 1

Subject & Content of Art

Basic Components of a Work of Art

👁️

Subject

The visual focus or image extracted from examining the artwork — the "What".

💬

Content

The meaning communicated by the artist or the artwork — the "Why".

🔧

Form

The development and configuration of the artwork; how elements and medium are put together — the "How".

Types of Subject

1. Representational / Objective Art

Represents objects or events in the real world. Easily recognizable, derived from real object sources with strong visual reference to reality.

Subcategories:

  • Still Life — e.g., Sunflowers (Van Gogh); Jug, Curtain and Fruit Bowl (Cézanne)
  • Portrait — e.g., Girl with a Pearl Earring (Vermeer); Mona Lisa (da Vinci)
  • Landscape — e.g., Water Lilies (Monet); The Hay Wain (Constable)

2. Non-Representational Art

Does not make reference to the real world — no person, place, thing, or event. Simply depicts shapes, lines, and colors to translate feelings, emotions, and concepts.

Example: Composition VIII by Wassily Kandinsky

What is Content in Art?

Content refers to the meaning, message, or "why" behind a work. While the subject is what you physically see, the content is the emotional or intellectual significance the artist intends to communicate.

"Content is inextricably linked with Form." It is the mass of ideas communicated through:

  • The art's imagery
  • The symbolic meaning
  • Its surroundings where it is used or displayed
  • The customs, beliefs, and values of the culture

Three Levels of Meaning

1. Factual Meaning

The literal statement or narrative content directly apprehended because objects are easily recognized. Example: "The painting shows a woman sitting by a window."

2. Conventional Meaning

Widely accepted interpretation of specific signs, symbols, or motifs within a particular culture, time period, or group. Example: A cross representing Christianity.

3. Subjective Meaning

Interpretation arising from the viewer's personal experiences, emotions, or values, as well as the artist's private symbolic intent.

03Visual Art

Forms of Visual Art

Painting

Applying pigment to a surface (canvas, wood, paper, wall) using brushes, knives, or sponges. One of the oldest and most recognized art forms.

Drawing

Creating images using pencils, charcoal, ink, or pastels. Considered the foundation of visual arts. Focuses on line, shading, and form.

Sculpture

Three-dimensional artworks created by carving, modeling, casting, or assembling materials.

Printmaking

Creating artwork by printing from a prepared surface. Allows multiple copies of the same design.

Photography

Capturing images using light-sensitive materials or digital sensors. Used for art, journalism, and documentation.

Digital Art

Art created using digital technology. Includes digital painting, 3D modeling, and generative art.

Architecture

Design and construction of buildings — combines beauty and function.

Ceramics

Creating objects from clay, fired at high temperatures.

Textile Arts

Art made using fabric, fiber, and thread. Includes weaving, embroidery, quilting, and tapestry.

Mosaic

Creating images by assembling small pieces of colored glass, stone, or tile. Often used in architecture and decoration.

Collage

Assembling different materials (paper, photographs, fabric, found objects) onto a surface to create a new composition.

Installation Art

Large-scale, mixed-media constructions designed to transform a space and create an immersive experience.

Performance Art

Art in which the artist's actions constitute the artwork, often combining visual elements with movement and time.

Video Art

Art using moving images and sound displayed on screens or projected, exploring narrative and non-narrative forms.

Illustration

Creating visual representations to accompany or explain text — used in books, magazines, advertising, and comics.

Calligraphy

The art of beautiful writing, treating letters and text as visual design elements.

Glass Art

Creating artwork from glass through blowing, casting, staining, or fusing.

Mixed Media

Combining multiple artistic techniques and materials in a single artwork.

Street Art / Graffiti

Art created in public spaces — spray-painted murals, stencils, wheatpastes, and installations.

Land Art / Earth Art

Large-scale interventions in natural landscapes, using earth, rocks, water, and other natural materials.

04Creators of Art

Artists & Artisans

An artist is generally defined as an art practitioner — a painter, sculptor, actor, writer, poet, musician, and the like — who produces or creates indirectly functional arts with aesthetic value using imagination.

Types of Artists

Painter

Creates images using paint on surfaces like canvas, paper, walls, or wood. Uses colors, shapes, and techniques to express ideas, emotions, or scenes.

Example: Leonardo da Vinci — Mona Lisa, The Last Supper

Sculptor

Creates three-dimensional works by carving, molding, or assembling materials like stone, metal, wood, or clay.

Example: Michelangelo — David, Pietà, Sistine Chapel

Musician

Creates or performs music using instruments, voice, or digital tools. Expresses emotions and ideas through sound, rhythm, and melody.

Example: Ludwig van Beethoven — Symphony No. 5 (struggle and victory)

Writer

Creates stories, poems, articles, or other texts using words to express ideas, emotions, or information.

Example: William Shakespeare — Hamlet, Romeo and Juliet, Macbeth

Actor

Portrays characters in plays, movies, or TV shows using speech, body language, and emotions to bring stories to life.

Artisan

A skilled craftsperson who creates objects by hand using traditional techniques, focusing on both functionality and aesthetic quality. Products reflect cultural traditions.

Filipino Artisans

Weaving

Interlacing threads or fibers to create fabric. Examples: Inabel (Ilocos — cotton with geometric patterns), Malong (Mindanao — abaca cloth).

Pottery / Ceramics

Shaping and firing clay to create functional and decorative objects. Uses local clay and traditional firing techniques.

Woodcarving

Shaping and decorating wood through carving techniques. Famous example: Paete, Laguna woodcarvers.

Metal & Jewelry

Crafting metals into ornaments and functional items — necklaces, bracelets, rings, and tools — reflecting cultural identity. Kalinga and Mindanao artisans.

Basketry

Weaving and crafting natural materials like bamboo, rattan, and palm leaves to create baskets and everyday items. Rural and indigenous Filipino artisans.

05History of Art

Art Movements Through the Ages

Art in Early Civilizations

Stone Age / Paleolithic Era
Cave Art
Found in caves from early prehistoric societies. Made using natural pigments. Subjects: animals, hunting scenes, human figures. Focused on survival, rituals, and communication. Considered advanced for their time. Examples: Hall of Bulls (Lascaux); Cueva de las Manos; Venus of Willendorf; Stonehenge
Ancient Egypt — 2686–1069 BCE
Egyptian Art
Old Kingdom: Pyramids of Giza, Narmer Palette. Middle Kingdom: Portrait sculptures, rock-cut tombs, fresco paintings. New Kingdom / Amarna Period: King Akhenaten and Queen Nefertiti led a revolution (capital moved to Tel El-Amarna). Tutankhamun's Tomb.
Ancient Greece
Greek Art — Four Periods
Greeks valued poetry, drama, and philosophy. Art required skill sets and body of knowledge. Theater originated with followers of Dionysus. Periods: Geometric (Dipylon Amphora — funerary vase with geometric patterns), Archaic (Kouros Statues — rigid, Egyptian-influenced, Archaic smile), Classical (Parthenon — dedicated to Athena, peak of Greek art), Hellenistic (Laocoon and His Sons — intense emotion and drama).
Ancient Rome — c. 500 BCE
Roman Art
Established around 500 BCE. Came of age during the Hellenistic Period. Romans admired Greek achievements — fusion of Greek and Roman culture. Known for realism and grand monuments. Examples: Colosseum (holds ~50,000 spectators); Perseus and Medusa (Benvenuto Cellini)
Middle Ages — c. 500–1500
Medieval Art
Period between decline of the Roman Empire and the Renaissance. Church was the central figure. Two cathedral styles: Romanesque (1050–1200) inspired by Rome (e.g., Pisa Cathedral); Gothic (1200–1500) with pointed arches, flying buttresses, stained glass (e.g., Notre Dame Cathedral).
Renaissance — c. 1400–1600
Renaissance Art
Artists valued the "individual." Humanism shifted focus to empower the individual. Emphasized naturalism and proportionality of the human body. Non-religious themes also explored. Revival of Roman theatrical plays. Examples: Mona Lisa and The Last Supper (da Vinci); David (Michelangelo)
c. 1520–1600
Mannerism
Product of the Renaissance. Artists began copying from existing works rather than nature. Characterized by distorted figures, two-dimensional spaces, discordant colors, and lack of defined focal point. Example: Madonna with Long Neck (Parmigianino) — very long neck and elongated limbs.
Baroque — 1600–1750
Baroque & Rococo Art
From Portuguese "barocco" — irregularly shaped pearl. Birthplace: Rome, seen as a response to Protestantism. Colorful palettes, ornamentation, and dramatic emotion. Music also flourished — Bach, Handel, Vivaldi. Rococo: From French "rocaille." Light, ornate, playful — soft pastels, C/S curves, aristocratic pleasure. Examples: The Assumption of the Virgin; The Four Seasons (Vivaldi); The Swing (Rococo)
Late 18th–Early 19th Century
Neoclassicism
Emerged partly from the French Revolution (1789). Aimed to revive Greek and Roman influences. A reaction against Baroque extravagance — aligned with Enlightenment principles. Example: Paolina Borghese as Venus Victrix
19th Century
Romanticism
Used Neoclassicism as a springboard. Highlighted heroic elements, nationalistic movements. Focus on goodness of mankind, justice, equality, social order, emotions, and feelings. Example: The Raft of the Medusa — human suffering, heroism, and desperation.

Literary Arts

Literary arts is a form of creative writing such as poems, stories, and essays that express a person's thoughts, feelings, and experiences. They include academic forms such as essays, reports, and research papers — important not only for creativity and entertainment but also for education and learning.

Elements of Literary Arts

Plot

The sequence of events that drive the story.

Character

The people (or animals, etc.) in the story, with their own traits and motivations.

Setting

The time and place where the story happens.

Theme

The underlying message or idea the writer is trying to convey.

Style

The writer's unique voice, tone, and language use.

Point of View

Who's telling the story (e.g., first person, third person).

Symbolism

Objects, colors, etc. that represent bigger ideas.

Imagery

Language that appeals to the senses and creates vivid pictures.

Classification of Literary Arts

Poetry

A form using aesthetic and rhythmic qualities of language, often in lines and stanzas.

Narrative Poetry

Tells a story with a plot, characters, setting, and defined beginning, middle, and end. Examples: The Iliad, The Odyssey (Homer)

Metrical Poetry

Structured by a consistent, rhythmic pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables (meter). Example: Shakespeare's sonnets

Free Verse

An open form that does not use consistent meter, rhyme, or musical rhythm. Example: Modern poetry collections

Prose

The most common form of writing, using natural language, standard grammar, and conversational flow arranged in sentences and paragraphs.

Fiction

Literature in prose describing imaginary events and people.

  • Novels — Long fictional narrative. Pride and Prejudice (Austen)
  • Short Stories — 1,000–20,000 words, read in one sitting. The Cask of Amontillado (Poe)
  • Fables — Short story teaching a moral lesson; animals with human traits. The Boy Who Cried Wolf (Aesop)

Non-Fiction

Based on facts, real events, and actual people. Intended to inform, teach, or document reality.

  • Biographies — Life of a person, written by someone else. Alexander Hamilton (Ron Chernow)
  • Autobiographies — Person writes about their own life. Diary of a Young Girl (Anne Frank)
  • Essays — Author's argument or analysis on a topic. Politics and the English Language (Orwell)

Drama

A representation through dialogue and performance — a story acted out on stage, film, or radio.

07Theatrical Art

Theater Arts

Theater Arts is a collaborative form of fine art that uses live performers to present the experience of a real or imagined event before a live audience. It features live interaction, is multidisciplinary (acting, directing, design, technical elements), and aims to convey a story or evoke specific emotions.

History of Theater

Ancient Roots

Began with Greek dramatic festivals honoring Dionysus — the foundation of Western theater.

Roman Influence

Adapted Greek traditions; focused on entertainment, stage design, and spectacle.

Medieval Period

Mystery and Morality plays shifted from religious to secular themes. Theater stages became smaller.

Renaissance

Revival of classics, famously led by playwrights like William Shakespeare.

19th Century

Realism emerged as a dominant theatrical style.

20th Century – Today

Experimental and avant-garde movements emerged, pushing boundaries. Theater continues to evolve with diverse styles.

Core Elements of Theater

1. Acting

The "cornerstone" of theater. Performers embody characters through dialogue, movement, and emotion.

2. Directing

The unifying vision of the production. The Director guides actors and designers to ensure a cohesive performance.

3. Plot

The sequence of events. Typically follows a Beginning, Middle, and End to allow for character growth.

4. Set Design

The physical environment on stage. Uses scenery, backdrops, and props to create atmosphere and context.

5. Characters

Protagonists (main hero), Antagonists (opposition), and Foils. Introduced early in the script.

6. Costume Design

Visual storytelling through clothing. Defines character's personality, social status, and time period.

7. Lighting & Sound

Technical tools that create mood, highlight focal points, and immerse the audience.

8. Script

The written foundation of the performance. A blueprint guiding actors and technical crews.

Audience Engagement & Conventions

Fourth Wall

The invisible boundary between performers and audience. Can be broken for dramatic effect.

Interactive / Participatory

Audiences become part of the performance.

Dramatic Convention

Shared theatrical rules that shape how stories are told. Examples: Monologue, Aside, Freeze Frame.

Types of Theater

Traditional Theater

Classical plays, musicals, and operas adhering to established conventions.

Experimental Theater

Pushes boundaries of traditional forms with unconventional narratives and avant-garde techniques.

Physical Theater

Emphasizes the body as primary storytelling tool. Combines movement, gesture, and expression.

Interactive Theater

Engages the audience directly, blurring lines between performers and spectators.

Multimedia Theater

Incorporates audiovisual effects, projections, and digital media to enhance the experience.

Stage Types

Proscenium Stage

Traditional framed stage with audience seated in front.

Thrust Stage

Audience seated on three sides. Stage extends into auditorium.

Theater-in-the-Round

Audience seated all around the performance area.

Black Box / Flexible

Adaptable space for creative staging.

Music — The Art of Organized Sound

Music is an art form that combines vocal or instrumental sounds designed for aesthetic experience, emotional impact, and cultural meaning. While nature provides noise, humanity provides the structure.

Elements of Music

Rhythm

The pattern of time. The duration and placement of sounds. Long sounds, short sounds, rest.

Melody

The tune. A linear succession of musical tones — the main tune of a song, what you remember when you hum.

Harmony

The vertical structure. Simultaneous notes played together. e.g., choir, guitar and piano playing at the same time.

Timbre

The tone quality or "color." What makes it sound different from other instruments or voices. (e.g., Ariana Grande vs. Adele singing the same note)

Dynamics

The variation in loudness or softness (intensity). e.g., Bohemian Rhapsody moves from soft piano to loud operatic sections.

Criteria of Music as Art

Aesthetic Intent

Created specifically for an aesthetic experience and emotional impact, rather than purely functional utility.

Cultural Standards

The norms, traditions, and expectations within a culture that guide how music is created, performed, and evaluated.

Historical Continuity

The preservation and transmission of artistic, cultural, or social traditions over time across generations.

Global Evolution of Music

3000 BCE – 500 BCE
Ancient Civilization
Served primarily religious, ceremonial, and social functions. Instruments: lyres, harps, flutes, drums. Vocal chants were the main element. Example: Hurrian Hymn No. 6 (c. 1400 BCE) — oldest known melody, played with lyre.
500–1400 CE
Medieval
Dominated by the Christian Church. Monophonic Gregorian chants. Instruments: lute, vielle, organ. Gradual evolution of musical notation. Example: Gregorian Chant
1400–1600
Renaissance
Flourishing of harmony and vocal polyphony. More expressive and structured. Counterpoint and balanced textures. Instruments: lute, viol, recorder. Music increasingly printed. Example: "Ave Maria" by Josquin des Prez
1600–1750
Baroque
Emphasized ornamentation, contrast, and emotional expression. Rise of instrumental forms: fugue, concerto, opera. Instruments: harpsichord, violin, organ. Example: Brandenburg Concerto No. 3 (Bach, 1721)
1750–1820
Classical
Valued clarity, balance, and structural form (reaction against Baroque complexity). Developed symphonies, sonatas, and string quartets. Performed in courts, salons, and public concert halls. Example: Symphony No. 40 (Mozart, 1788)
1800–1900
Romantic
Reaction against rationalism and Industrial Revolution social changes. Emotional expression, dramatic storytelling, national identity. Expanded orchestras. Inspired by literature, nature, or history. Example: Symphonie Fantastique (Berlioz, 1830)
20th Century – Present
Modern
Experimentation, innovation, and diversity. New scales, electronic instruments, digital sound. Led to jazz, rock, and electronic music. Example: "Tadhana" by Up Dharma Down (2012)

Music in the Philippine Context

Indigenous Roots

Deeply connected to daily life, rites, and rituals. Functional, spiritual, and communal. Kulintang Gong Ensemble — reflects ancient traditions; a marker of indigenous identity.

Spanish Influence

Introduction of Western melody and Christianity blended with local traditions. Harana (Serenade) — courtship tradition fusing indigenous customs and Hispanic musical structures.

American Influence & OPM

Formal education and pop genres evolved into Original Pilipino Music. Kundiman — elevated by Francisco Santiago & Nicanor Abelardo; expresses Filipino sentimentality and patriotism.

09Dance as Art

Dance

Dance is a performing art that uses intentional and rhythmic body movement to express emotion, ideas, and culture. It is considered art because it communicates meaning through movement, creativity, and interpretation. It is one of the oldest art forms in human history.

Elements of Dance

Body

The instrument used to create movement. Using arms to create shapes, isolating shoulders, contracting the torso.

Action

Movements such as jumping, turning, sliding, and gesturing. Leaping, spinning in a pirouette, sliding into a floor move.

Space

Direction, level, pathway, and positioning in the performance area. Traveling diagonally, dancing at low level, or forming pathways.

Time

Rhythm, tempo, and beat of the movement. Moving slowly to a ballad, hitting sharp beats in hip-hop, or flowing with jazz rhythms.

Energy

The force and quality of movement. A strong, explosive jump versus a soft, fluid arm wave; sharp staccato gestures versus smooth flow.

History of Dance

Ancient Period
Ritual & Ceremonial Dance
Ritual dances, Greek maenad dances, Bharatanatyam (India). Symbolic, often tied to religion and mythology. Used in ceremonies, storytelling, worship, and community bonding.
Renaissance Period
Court Dances
Pavane, Minuet, Galliard, Masques. Structured, elegant, and refined movements. Entertainment in royal courts, social gatherings, and festivals.
Modern Period
Contemporary Dance Forms
Ballet, Jazz, Contemporary, Hip-Hop. Technical precision, expressive freedom, and rhythmic energy. Focuses on artistic performance, cultural expression, social identity, and innovation.

Types of Dance

Folk Dance

Traditional dance representing the culture, history, and customs of a specific community or country. In the Philippines, folk dances show hospitality, courtship traditions, and rural life.

Ballet

A highly technical and formal style originating during the Renaissance in Europe. Combines movement, music, costume, and stage design to create a complete theatrical experience.

Modern & Contemporary Dance

Developed as a reaction against the strict rules of ballet. Emphasizes freedom of movement and personal expression.

Social & Street Dance

Developed in communities, performed in social settings or public spaces. Emphasizes individuality, freestyle movement, and rhythm.

Dance as Art — Three Functions

Emotional Expression

Communicates emotion without the use of words.

Cultural Storytelling

Tells stories, traditions, and cultural values.

Reflection of Identity

Reflects identity and shared human experience.

Importance of Dance in Culture

  • Preserves cultural traditions and rituals
  • Encourages creativity and self-expression
  • Strengthens community connection and cultural identity
10Architectural Arts

Architecture

Architecture is the art and science of designing and constructing buildings and structures. It combines Creativity (Art), Technical Knowledge (Engineering), Functionality (Use), and Aesthetic Value (Beauty). Its purposes: provide shelter, create functional spaces, express culture and identity, improve quality of life.

Elements of Architectural Design

Line

Defines edges and structure; guides the eye. Vertical = strength; Horizontal = calmness; Diagonal = movement/energy.

Shape

Two-dimensional area. Geometric: square, rectangle, circle. Organic: natural, irregular shapes.

Form

Three-dimensional shape (cube, sphere, cylinder). Gives buildings volume and creates structure.

Space

Positive space = occupied. Negative space = empty. Rooms, hallways, open areas.

Texture

Surface quality (smooth glass, rough concrete, wood, brick). Adds visual interest and enhances appearance.

Color

Improves appearance, creates mood, adds beauty. White = clean/simple; Dark = strong/formal; Bright = energetic/lively.

Principles of Architecture

Balance

Equal distribution of visual weight. Symmetrical or Asymmetrical. Makes buildings look stable. Example: Taj Mahal

Proportion

Relationship between sizes of parts. Ensures harmony and realistic design. Proper sizing of doors, windows, walls.

Rhythm

Repetition of elements. Creates movement, pattern, and flow. Examples: Repeating windows, repeating columns.

Emphasis

Highlights the most important part. Attracts attention, creates a focal point. Examples: Main entrance, tower, dome (Eiffel Tower).

Unity

All parts work together. Creates harmony; building looks complete. Same colors, materials, style. Example: Sydney Opera House

  • Human Experience — Influences how people live, work, and interact by designing spaces that affect mood, productivity, and comfort.
  • Cultural Expression — Buildings and cities express a society's history, values, and traditions.

  • Health & Well-being — Good design promotes health, accessibility, natural light, and ventilation.
  • Innovation — Integrates new materials, engineering, and smart technologies.
  • Inspiration — Iconic architecture can inspire people, foster creativity, and become symbols of national pride.
11Spatial Representation

Planes & Perspectives in Art

Planes in Art

A plane is a flat, two-dimensional surface that extends in height and width. In art, planes help organize space and create the illusion of depth.

Picture Plane

The flat surface of the artwork (canvas, paper, wall, etc.).

Foreground Plane

The part of the artwork closest to the viewer.

Middle Ground Plane

The area between foreground and background.

Background Plane

The area farthest from the viewer.

Perspectives in Art

Perspective in art is the technique artists use to create the illusion of depth and three-dimensional space on a flat surface. One major type is linear perspective, which uses lines that converge at a vanishing point.

Linear Perspective (Popular during the Renaissance)

One-Point Perspective

Creates illusion of depth using one vanishing point on the horizon line. All lines moving away from the viewer converge toward that single point.

Two-Point Perspective

Uses two vanishing points on the horizon line. Viewer sees the object from an angle, usually showing the corner of a building or object.

Three-Point Perspective

Uses three vanishing points — two on the horizon plus one above or below — for a more dramatic and realistic sense of depth.

Other Perspective Techniques

Atmospheric (Aerial) Perspective

Creates depth using color and clarity rather than converging lines. Distant objects appear: Lighter, Bluer, and Less detailed.

Foreshortening

Makes parts of the body or objects appear shorter or compressed to show depth. Creates the illusion that the object extends toward or away from the viewer.
